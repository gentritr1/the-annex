## Anti-pattern verdict

The interface does **not** read as generic AI product UI. The restrained amber/cyan semantics, flat registry surfaces, credible municipal copy, coherent sigils, and absence of purple-neon/glass/card-grid spectacle give it a specific identity. The generated environment image is visually strong; its reuse is what exposes asset scarcity.

The deterministic Impeccable scan covered 25 files under `src`, exited 0, and returned `[]`: zero rule findings and no false positives. No browser overlay was produced because the available Browser evaluation path is read-only and cannot inject the detector script. Independent live desktop and 390px inspection supplied the visual fallback.

## Overall Impression

The Annex now has a compelling facade, a coherent deterministic game, and the beginning of a real campaign. Its biggest opportunity is not more visual effects. It is to make each case feel like entering a different authored place and performing a different kind of inquiry. Today the title is cinematic, but the middle becomes a refined text ledger and Case 81 feels like excellent new writing inside Case 77's skin.

## What's Working

- **The opening promise is excellent.** Severe civic architecture, rain, controlled amber service light, and restrained cyan records match “municipal control room after midnight” without collapsing into generic cyberpunk.
- **State and consequence are trustworthy.** The reducer owns canonical effects, every action previews a cost, the rail shows evidence/trust/alarm, local save migration is tested, and cross-case history is real.
- **The storyline inversion is strong.** Case 77 asks whether a prohibited restoration continues Mara; Case 81 asks whether the city can manufacture a useful witness and then certify utility as personhood. The verdict-specific Case 77 citations in Case 81 are the campaign's best cross-case writing.

## Priority Issues

### [P1] Shared runtime semantics still contain Case-77-only IDs

**Why it matters:** Case 81's `seal-certification` is not tagged or styled as an illicit finding because the engine and tribunal only special-case `overwrite-record`. Its `standing-deadlock` reconstruction also misses the explicit warning treatment reserved for `unresolved-composite`. These are shipped state-communication defects even though all 70 tests pass.

**Fix:** Add explicit authored semantics to the existing definitions—for example decision method tags/tone and reconstruction outcome tone—then render those fields. This is a proven two-case variation, so the small extraction is now justified. Add Case 81 assertions for the forged seal and deadlocked model.

**Suggested command:** `$impeccable harden`

### [P1] Case 81 is an authored inversion but a mechanical and visual reskin

**Why it matters:** Both cases use the same four approaches, four binary sites, lattice, four findings, recurring cast, image, crops, and list rhythm. The prose changes, but the player's embodied experience barely does.

**Fix:** Give Case 81 unique environment art and replace at least one repeated grammar. A deposition transcript with interrupt/withhold/corroborate actions, or a witness-consent scene, would make the inversion playable. Preserve shared reducers only where the domain is actually shared.

**Suggested command:** `$impeccable shape`

### [P1] The “3D world” is a non-interactive 2.5D title treatment

**Why it matters:** The title has four CSS-transformed 2D planes and about one degree of pointer tilt. Briefing and investigation add Canvas 2D rain to the same static image; the investigation labels are inert `div`s; reconstruction, tribunal, and debrief have no Atmosphere. Players see a world but cannot use it.

**Fix:** Build an accessible authored diorama rather than a free-roaming engine. Make map hotspots real buttons linked to site records; focusing a hotspot should shift crop/depth, reveal site-specific foregrounds, and scroll to the canonical DOM choice. Keep the full list for keyboard, touch, and assistive technology.

**Suggested command:** `$impeccable overdrive`

### [P2] Atmosphere disappears during the emotional middle and climax

**Why it matters:** Once the map scrolls away, the longest portion becomes repeated text rows. Reconstruction and tribunal—the conceptual and emotional peaks—lose place entirely. Rain is one unchanging visual vocabulary, and there is no soundscape.

**Fix:** Carry a narrow world window through reconstruction, tribunal, and debrief; author case/phase presentation manifests for crop, foreground, weather, and lighting; make state changes affect restrained environmental cues. Add opt-in layered audio after a user gesture, with independent mute/volume and visibility pausing.

**Suggested command:** `$impeccable delight`

### [P2] Cross-case memory changes interpretation more than play, while 81-C lacks agency

**Why it matters:** Precedents unlock Case 81, alter Mirror/tribunal copy, and contribute bounded trust residue. They do not change a site, evidence source, witness behavior, lawful finding, or cost. Meanwhile 81-C remains “it,” never gives the crucial testimony onscreen, and cannot choose whether to testify.

**Fix:** Make each prior ruling alter one playable condition. Add a lawful Case 81 finding that recognizes 81-C as a person while striking the commissioned testimony until the person independently consents to speak. Give 81-C a chosen name, direct voice, desire, and an onscreen answer.

**Suggested command:** `$impeccable craft`

## Persona Red Flags

**Jordan, first-time narrative player:** The opening is legible, but “run,” “case,” “residual signal,” “memory lattice,” and the case rail arrive with little orientation. The title still markets “Playable systems prototype · Case 77” even after a second case exists. Jordan may understand each button yet miss the campaign model.

**Mina, returning story-focused player:** The Case 81 premise promises a witness and the author of the fourth minute, but Mina encounters the same exterior and list structure, never hears the testimony, never learns the named author onscreen, and cannot let the witness separate personhood from compelled use.

**Sam, motion-sensitive/low-vision player:** Reduced motion, high contrast, larger text, focus management, and 44px controls are strong. However, reduced motion removes weather entirely instead of providing a static atmospheric fallback, programmatic heading focus draws a large cyan rectangle, and two of four map labels disappear below 460px.

## Minor Observations

- Programmatic phase focus plus `h1:focus-visible` produces a conspicuous cyan box around every hero heading. Preserve focus transfer, but use a non-editorial visual treatment for programmatically focused headings.
- Investigation displays “Precipitation masking: 12%” while it passes a `0.07` mask.
- Documentation says five planes, but four plane nodes are rendered.
- `docs/PROVENANCE.md` still says React ambience integration is pending.
- The debrief uses a generic `◌` instead of the authored Mirror sigil.
- Mobile hides the care/archive map labels instead of repositioning all four.
- Canvas DPR is capped, hidden/offscreen loops pause, and cleanup is good; add a backing-pixel ceiling or 30fps mode for high-resolution displays.
- Campaign canon needs a rule before more cases: does replay replace the official precedent, or is it a Mirror simulation? Currently the latest replay rewrites the precedent map.

This develops the arc as: **individual identity → instrumental personhood → testimony and culpability → public policy → institutional resistance → judgment of the system itself.**

## Questions to Consider

1. Is “3D” meant to become a gameplay mechanic, or should the product own a more achievable identity as an authored spatial dossier/diorama?
2. Should a first verdict become campaign canon while replays are Mirror simulations, or should timeline rewriting be an explicit mechanic with downstream consequences?
3. Is Case 81 willing to let 81-C refuse testimony after receiving personhood? If not, the story risks reproducing the city's false premise instead of letting the player challenge it.
